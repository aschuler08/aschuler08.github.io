Agricultural Zones

What are Agricultural Zones?

North Texas falls primarily within **USDA Hardiness Zones 7b, 8a, and 8b**. These zones are defined by the average annual minimum winter temperature and are mapped by the USDA. * **Zone 7b**: Average minimum temperatures of 5–10°F * **Zone 8a**: 10–15°F * **Zone 8b**: 15–20°F These zones matter because they determine which plants can reliably survive winter in a specific area. For gardeners, landscapers, and farmers in North Texas, choosing plants suited to the correct zone helps prevent cold damage, reduces plant loss, and improves long-term success. Beyond winter lows, North Texas also experiences intense summer heat, clay-heavy soils, drought cycles, and occasional ice storms. Understanding your zone allows for better crop planning, planting schedules, and plant selection—especially for fruit trees, perennials, and native plants. Being aware of your agricultural zone helps you plant smarter, conserve resources, and create a more resilient North Texas garden or farm. 🌿

To learn determine your specific zone, you can use the USDA Plant Hardiness Zone Map or consult local extension services for microclimate information.
